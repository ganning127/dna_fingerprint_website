DNA Fingerprinting: Identifying a person from their DNA

tl;dr

In order for DNA fingerprinting to work, the DNA needs to contain a sequence that is unique in its DNA. Used to identify bodies, track down blood relatives, paternity disputes, and more.

How does DNA Fingerprint identify a person?

DNA fingerprinting focuses on sequences of DNA called minisatellites that contain repeating patterns with no known function.

Minisatellites (AKA tandem repeats) - repetitive DNA sequences (10-60bp) that are repeated around 5-60 times in the entire genome.

Minisatellites have a rich G-C base pair content and a high mutation rate.

Therefore, a person is likely to have a similar number of repeats to their close relatives than people more distantly related to them and strangers.

Minisatellites are present in the centromeres and the telomeres of chromosomes. They also regulate transcription and splicing of genes

STRs (includes minisatellites) appear in the non-coding regions of the DNA (don’t encode proteins), so mutations appear here much more often than in other parts of the genome.

Use in Crime Scenes

Almost every single cell in a multicellular organism contains the full set of DNA required for that organism (its complete genome) This is why when blood is left behind at a crime scene, we are able to use the blood to get the entire DNA. This DNA can later be used to perform DNA fingerprinting to determine the identity of the person.

Process of DNA Fingerprinting in Real Life

  1. A DNA sample is taken (mouth swab, blood sample, a swab from the crime scene, etc) is taken and purified
  2. In order to separate out the tandem repeats, the sample is put through PCR
  3. PCR copies and amplifies specific target DNA sequences, which in this case are STRs, increasing their relative numbers in the sample
  4. The different lengths copied during the PCR reaction are then separated out using electrophoresis.
  5. DNA is added to an agarose gel and an electric current is run through it.
  6. Since DNA is negatively charged, the DNA will move through the gel towards the other end of the gel.
  7. Longer sequences will not move far as much as shorter sequences.
  8. Human inspection is able to match the result on the gel to the known gel results in a database, effectively identifying the pereson.
